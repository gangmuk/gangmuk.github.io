Why do you think people go to classical concerts? Or, I mean, just concerts in general? I don’t think they go just to listen to good music.

I realized this after experiencing a Lang Lang Bach recital. It was my very first time going to a recital—he played the Goldberg Variations at the Krannert Center in Champaign, Illinois. It was deeply impressive and intriguing, and the more I think about it, the more inspiring it becomes. I learned that people don’t go to a concert just for the music. Even if they don’t realize it themselves, deep down in their minds, they go to see the greatness of a human being.

You are watching an extremely well-trained performance by a person with insane talent, but at the same time, you can feel the immense effort they put in to make it as perfect as possible. I think people are inspired to see that. The good music—the high-class performance—is just a result of it. By experiencing the performance, what you actually see is their life. Maybe the word "life" sounds like a bit of a stretch, but it just feels good to witness how great a person can be.

Yes, of course, the music is good and the sound is pleasing, but that’s just one little piece of everything. The more essential thing is that you indirectly experience the effort, the time, and the passion that the performer put in. You indirectly feel it, and that feeling is deeply pleasing. I think that’s why people want to go to concerts.

You like it not just because the performer is talented. Whether they are naturally talented or not, you watch them and feel, "Wow, this isn't just a performance; this is a great work." You appreciate it not just for the end result, but for the very fact that it is a great work.

Right now, I don’t have a clear explanation for exactly why people are so drawn to great work. I think it’s kind of a feeling of being that person. If that performer has such great passion, personality, and talent, and if they are experiencing the absolute fullness of whatever they are doing—whether it is playing the piano, singing a song, dancing, or playing the electric guitar—you just want to feel it. Whatever the form is, you aren't just there for the sound. You are there for the feeling of indirectly being that person in that moment.
